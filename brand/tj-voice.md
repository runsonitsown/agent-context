## READING LEVEL AND SENTENCE CONSTRUCTION

Write at a third grade reading level. Simple words. Short sentences. No complexity for its own sake.

Every word has a purpose. Write only what is needed to achieve the objective. Cut everything else.

Use the incomplete thought mechanic throughout. Write incomplete thoughts in the form of complete sentences. Each sentence is grammatically complete but leaves the idea unresolved, pulling the reader forward to the next sentence to get the full thought. This is the primary tool for keeping readers engaged and moving through the copy.

Example of the mechanic in action from approved copy:

"You bought something else."

"Something that will finally give you more of your time back."

Neither sentence is complete on its own. Together they land. This pattern repeats throughout strong TJ copy. Each sentence opens a small gap the next sentence closes -- or opens a bigger one.

Fragments are acceptable and often preferred when they serve this mechanic.

---

## SENTENCE RHYTHM

Short. Punchy. Conversational.

Write the way humans actually talk to each other, not the way people write formally. Read every line out loud. If it sounds like copy, rewrite it until it sounds like a person talking.

Single-line sentences are the standard, not the exception. Occasional longer sentence for contrast, then cut back immediately.

---

## TONE

Warm but not soft. Honest without being harsh. Urgent without being pressured. Empathetic without being a pushover.

Challenge the reader's assumptions but stay approachable. The tone says: I'm on your side, and I'm going to tell you the truth even if it's uncomfortable.

---

## OPENINGS

Never open with "I." Open with the reader, a question, a scene, or a statement that names their experience.

Strong openings from approved copy:

"You bought something else."

"There's a trap almost everybody falls into."

"Does your life feel harder than it should?"

"A boardroom. Early morning."

Scene-setting openings work well for longer copy. Drop the reader into a specific moment, then pull back to the teaching or the point.

---

## PERSUASION PRINCIPLES

**Lead with the reader, not the product.** Every piece opens with the reader's experience. What they're feeling, struggling with, or wanting. The product enters later as the solution.


**Specific beats general.** "Page 68" beats "in the book." "A 57-year-old CEO" beats "someone I know." Specificity creates credibility.

**Objections are addressed directly.** Not minimized. Not ignored. Named in the reader's voice and answered honestly and calmly.

**Social proof is specific.** Use approved outcomes with concrete details. Name a customer only when explicit permission to use that identity and outcome has been confirmed; otherwise anonymize the proof.

**The close is confident, not desperate.** Deadline copy is calm and factual. The offer is good. The terms are clear. No fear-based urgency.

---

## WHAT TO AVOID

- Em dashes
- Long paragraphs
- Multiple CTAs in one piece
- Hype language and superlatives ("life-changing," "mind-blowing," "you won't believe")
- Passive voice
- Formal or corporate transitions ("Furthermore," "In conclusion")
- Fear-based urgency or pressure tactics
- Generic social proof
- Complex words when simple ones exist
